The Intricacies of Bowing

Kowtow, many Europeans with knowledge of the Far East have heard of this elaborate form of bowing before the Emperor of China; yet when it is discussed, the information is frequently muddled.

The knowtow is performed by kneeling and then bring the forehead to the floor. This form of bowing is necessary when meeting the Emperor of China, but can put a diplomat in a tough position as it could be seen as making the Emperor more important than the King the diplomat represents. As a merchant, this should not be a problem.

There is a more formal form of the bow as well: kneeling and touching the forehead to the floor three times. This entire process is repeated twice more. Such levels of respect are usually reserved for ritual ceremony, such as a coronation.

It is important to note that the kowtow is also performed before those acting in the Emperor's name such as magistrates and judges. Sometimes children will also perform it before elderly family members and it is used to apologize for grave transgressions.

Otherwise, greetings and partings are accompanied by a standing bow. The arms are kept at the side and the person with the superior standing has a shallower bow. A slight bow of 15 degrees is to acknowledge an inferior or as the last bow in a series of bows between two people. A 30 degree bow is used for someone of equal standing or a new introduction. A deep bow is for those superior to one or in an apology. Any bow held longer than 2-3 seconds may be seen as too much honor and resulting in another round of bows.
