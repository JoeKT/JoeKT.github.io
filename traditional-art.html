Researching, testing, and sketching a design many times before committing to any involved and precise rendering will yield much better results. It's not just about subject and layout either; the sole purpose of this sketch was to test the interaction of acrylic paint, pastels, charcoal, and ink for compatability.

Perhaps my favorite medium, pen and ink allow me to focus on the details of a piece to a degree like no other. Of course such detail-oriented work requires a clear, strong understanding of the design before diving into the creating. Pen and ink also requires a firm grasp of different line types, weights, and textures, which is what I was practicing in this sketch.

Learning what tools and techniques to use for different effects is an important part of learning to design. I knew that painting wasn't a strong skill of mine, nor did it hold much interest to me. So when I was required to create an acrylic self-portrait, I turned to paint sticks. Still acrylic and allowing for the blending techniques of paint, these sticks allowed me to paint in a style which fit my skill set of drier media.

For this acrylic self-portrait I chose to paint with a palette knife instead of a brush; the bold, sharp strokes increased the emotional feel of the piece while providing a level of abstraction that complimented the unusual face I was making. Details and precision are important but sometimes they get in the way.

My first wireframing project! This plaster replica of my arm was built atop a mesh wire frame before being detailed with pastel, soder, and denim. While not directly related to wireframing for web apps or graphic design, training in 3-dimensional art helped foster my understanding of spacial relationships in design and expanded my studies of the positive/negative space dichotamy.
