The concern:

The colored circle (pictured) relays information about the difficulty of capturing the Pokemon via a culturally-common Red-Orange-Yellow-Green scale.

  1. This range is not accessible to to varying degrees for 8-10% of the male population.
  2. The surface area of the colored ring is too small to allow color-impaired users to use tint to determine its color easily.
  3. The small surface area of the color can blend in with some of the Pokemon, especially for those with color-impaired vision.

The Need:

A secondary source of the same information on difficulty that is easily identifiable. Such an addition need not be present by default but should always show once toggled on until toggled off.

Potential Solution: Shapes

The use of different shapes to replace the colored ring to denote difficult would alleviate the concern. However, it is not a perfect solution

  1. Shapes would only provide difficulty categories (ie, circle = easy, triangle = medium, square = difficult) while color allows for a continual gradient.

  2. Users recieve a bonus for landing a Pokeball within the colored area and different shapes for would affect the surface area available for that bonus.

Potential Solution: Meters

The use of meters allows the colored ring to remain unmodified while still addressing the need for greater clarity for some users. Such meters could take several different forms detailed below.

  1. The a bar meters would denote difficulty based on fullness.

  2. a2 shows how a "known" amount of difficulty could be displayed followed by a "possible" range. The actual difficult would lie somewhere between the beginning and end of the "possible" range. This could be used to prevent too much insight into the game's mechanics.

  3. a3 shows a much less precise "bar" method where as the difficulty increases, so do the amount of the objects filled in, in relation to the whole.

  4. The b radial meters would mark the outside of the stationary white ring present and increase in frequency in a clockwise fashion to denote increasing difficulty.

Potential Solution: Numerals

The use of a numerical value placed on the screen would provide the absolute clearest representation of difficulty. Depicted in the bottom-left of the mock-up, it could also be placed in the top-left or top-right without interfering with existing information, detracting from the focal point, or using valuable screen real estate in the comfortable interaction zone.

  1. Such definitive representation may be too much insight into the game's mechanics.

  2. Users would need to remember the range of difficulties, unless it were displayed as 9.8/10.

The three solutions above are part of six originally brainstormed. The remaining three were ruled out during the next round of iteration due to:

  • Thickness: adjusting the thickness of the colored ring based on difficulty had two main problems. First, it was not intuitive if a thicker ring was more or less difficult. Second, it significantly decreased the amount of the pokemon that was visible and as the ring shrank it became a filled circle rather than a ring.
  • White-Black: changing the red-orange-yellow-green spectrum to a white-grey-black spectrum did not really improve user understanding. It requires users to recall which shade of grey they are viewing and the "colored" surface area remains very small. Also, many of these other solutions would have a positive increase in clarity for users with other types of vision impairment but this would not.
  • Textual: Textual information would be customizable and thus allow for control over the precision of the information and also allow for it to be included in a thematically appropriate manner. However, it would not be native to the screen but need to be called up with a user action and then most likely require further actions to progress through and close. The addition of steps for users when the information could easily be displayed alternatively is a poor experience.
